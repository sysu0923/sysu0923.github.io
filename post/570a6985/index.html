Heart Failure with preserved ejection fraction: recent concepts in diagnosis, machanisms and management

发表于 2023-04-20 更新于 2023-04-21 分类于 临床文献 阅读次数： 0 本文字数： 11k 阅读时长 ≈ 10 分钟

Abstract

  1. One-half of all patients with heart failure (HF) have heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF).

  2. Among patients with HF, the proportion of hospitalizations and deaths due to cardiac causes decrease as LVEF increases.

  3. Medication classes that have been efficacious in HFrEF have been less so at higher LVEF ranges.

  4. These observations reflect the burden of non-cardiac comorbidities as LVEF increases.

  5. Treatment with sodium-glucose cotransporter two inhibitors reduce the risk of composite cardiovascular events, driven by reduced HF hospitalizations; renin-angiotensin-aldosterone blockers and angiotensin-neprilysin inhibitors result in a minor reduction in HF hospitalizations.

  6. Classification based on phenotypes** may facilitate a more targeted approach to
    treatment than LVEF categorization, which sets arbitrary cut-points when LVEF is a continuum.

The evolving definition of HFpEF

Recently re-labeled as ‘mildly reduced’ for LVEF 40-50%. HFpEF is now defined by normal LVEF (> 50%) along with signs and symptoms of heart failure after exclusion of prior reduced LVEF and treatable underlying conditions. However, these classifications of HF defined by arbitrary cut-points in LVEF do not appear consistent with recent evidence, which points to a gradual shift in underlying mechanisms, phenotypes, and response to therapy as LVEF increases, with considerable overlap.

Current diagnostic approach and its limitations

HFpEF can be defined theoretically as the presence of elevated left ventricular (LV) filling
pressure at rest or during exercise with normal LVEF. Most guidelines explain HFpEF clinically as (i) the presence of symptoms and signs of HF; (ii) an LVEF ≥ 50%, (iii) careful **exclusion of “HFpEF mimickers,**” and (iv) evidence of elevated left ventricular (LV)
filling pressure or non-invasive correlates (elevated E/e ratio, increased left atrial volume,
elevated natriuretic peptides [NP]).

Several “HFpEF” randomized clinical trials(RCTs) have used lower LVEF cut-offs (40-45%) to maximize event rates and thus attain statistical power.

NP levels may not always guide diagnosis as they tend to be lower in HFpEF than HFrEF, likely due to lower diastolic wall stress and a higher prevalence of obesity.

While these criteria rely on measurements at rest, left-sided filling pressures increase only with exercise in many patients with HFpEF; patients who do not experience dyspnea and demonstrate signs of elevated LV filling pressure at rest require advanced testing such as exercise echocardiography or invasive exercise hemodynamic to unmask abnormal diastolic reserve.

E/e’ during exercise has been shown to correlate reasonably well with invasively measured pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP).

Invasive hemodynamics directly measures PCPW during exercise, although even the diagnostic threshold is still debated (exercise PCWP≥25 mmHg or ΔPCWP/Δcardiac output slope>2.0 mmHg/L/min).

Two HFpEF diagnostic algorithms -the H2FPEF score and the European Society of Cardiology HFA-PEFF algorithm combine clinical characteristics and diagnostic parameters to distinguish HFpEF from non-cardiac dyspnea.

Validation of these scores against invasive hemodynamics demonstrated good performance: the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve was 0.73-0.74. Still, up to 23% of patients were misclassified in both scores, typically in patients with low scores who met invasive HFpEF criteria. Thus, low scores may not exclude HFpEF. Furthermore, a substantial proportion of patients have an intermediate probability for HFpEF using this classification system and require further testing.

HFpEF mimickers and specific cardiac aetiologies of HFpEF

“HFpEF mimickers” such as lung disease, pulmonary embolism, right-sided heart failure secondary to pulmonary hypertension, and renal failure can present similar symptoms and signs. These can be classified into diseases affecting the myocardium and altering cardiac loading conditions.

Recent Insights in HFpEF Pathophysiology

In a normal LV, volume increases during diastole are accompanied by minimal pressure increases due to enhanced diastolic suction; LV end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) remains regular with exercise.

In HFpEF, due to increased chamber stiffness, the diastolic pressure-volume relationship is shifted upwards and left compared to a normal LV. Volume changes thus lead to more significant increases in LVEDP.

Additionally, in most HFpEF patients, the exercise-induced increase in cardiac output is blunted due to poor contractile reserve and chronotropic incompetence. In overt HFpEF, the elevated LVEDP persists at rest, resulting in poor exercise tolerance.

Cardiac and non-cardiac mechanisms

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, sleep-disordered breathing, and lung parenchymal disease can result in pulmonary hypertension, eventually leading to RV failure.

Anemia is a common comorbidity, contributing to exercise intolerance and increased mortality.

Peripheral vascular dysfunction is frequent and is postulated to play a role in skeletal muscle dysfunction due to impaired oxygen delivery and extraction.

Chronic kidney disease is present in 50% of patients, and impaired fluid homeostasis in HFpEF is influenced by renal dysfunction.

Obesity is both a common comorbidity and a risk factor for developing HFpEF.

HFpEF phenotypes

As LVEF increases, patients with HF are more commonly women; more commonly have hypertension and atrial fibrillation; less commonly have ischemic heart disease; and have lower NP levels.

Lifestyle-based therapy

  1. In a small RCT in obese patients with HFpEF, a calorie restricted diet alone or in combination with exercise training(ET) was associated with significant weight loss and an increase in absolute peak oxygen consumption (VO2peak).
  2. A low sodium diet has been associated with favorable hemodynamic changes in HFpEF.
  3. ET has beneficial effects in HFpEF and associated comorbidities such as atrial fibrillation and coronary artery disease. (Both standard moderate continuous training to high intensity interval training). However, the benefit in VO2peak was not
    sustained during a 9-month home-based supervised extension of training    .
  4. A 6-month exercise program combining supervised and home-based aerobic exercise resulted in a significant decrease in the recurrence of atrial fibrillation and a reduction in symptom severity at 12 months.

Medical therapy

  1. To date, no medical therapy has demonstrated a reduction in CV death in trials of HFpEF, suggesting the relatively large burden of noncardiac mortality in this group.
  2. Despite a lack of robust evidence, diuretics have been the mainstay of HFpEF management and are recommended for relief of symptoms due to volume overload.
  3. Beta blockers are often prescribed in HFpEF to treat comorbidities such as coronary artery disease and atrial fibrillation. A meta-analysis of three medium-sized RCTs demonstrated a reduction in all-cause mortality, but a vast majority had LVEF <50%. Additionally, beta-blocker type may have differential effects across HFpEF phenotypes.
  4. Renin angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) have an established role in HFrEF, but have been less effective in HFpEF.
  5. Trials of angiotensin-converting enzyme receptor inhibitors (ACEi) and angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs) have failed to show a significant reduction in all- cause or CV death in HFpEF, but have decreased the risk of HF hospitalization.
  6. The Treatment of Preserved Cardiac Function HF With an Aldosterone Antagonist (TOPCAT) trial of the MRA spironolactone in HFpEF,failed to show an overall benefit in the primary composite outcome of CV death or HF hospitalization. However, in an exploratory analysis including only patients from the Americas, a small benefit on the primary outcome was noticed.
  7. In both TOPCAT and the TOPCAT-Americas subgroup, spironolactone was associated with a reduced risk of HF hospitalization.
  8. Machine learning analysis of the TOPCAT trial identified a phenotype characterized by obesity, diabetes, renal disease, and inflammation that exhibited higher CV risk and a better response to spironolactone treatment.
  9. The angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitor (ARNI) sacubitril-valsartan did not reduce the composite of CV death or total HF hospitalizations in patients with HF and LVEF ≥45% relative to valsartan in the Prospective Comparison of ARNI With ARB on Global Outcomes in HFpEF (PARAGON-HF) trial, but significantly reduced HF hospitalizations.
  10. Patients with elevated troponin or recent HF hospitalization were at higher CV risk and were more likely to benefit from ARNI. Also, benefits were higher in patients previously using MRA.
  11. These HFpEF phenotypes –more likely to be associated with structural heart disease and volume overload- may be more responsive to ARNI treatment.
  12. In the EMPEROR-Preserved trial, sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor (SGLT2i) empagliflozin reduced the risk of composite CV death or total HF hospitalization in HF with LVEF >40%. There was no statistically significant interaction between LVEF and the primary outcome in the trial, but benefit did not extend beyond LVEF of 60% in subgroup analysis.
  13. In the Effect of Sotagliflozin on Cardiovascular Events in Patients with Type 2 Diabetes Post Worsening Heart Failure (SOLOIST-WHF) trial, the dual SGLT2 and SGLT1 inhibitor sotagliflozin reduced the primary outcome of CV death and HF hospitalizations in patients with diabetes and worsening HF (both HFrEF and HFpEF), driven by reduced HF hospitalizations.

Sex and Racial differences

  1. Epidemiological studies and randomized trials demonstrate a female predominance in HFpEF (50-84%) due to differences in age and risk factors; adjusting for these differences males and females are at equal risk of developing HFpEF.
  2. In the TOPCAT-Americas trial, a post hoc analysis showed no sex differences in the primary composite outcome, but **females had a greater reduction in all-cause mortality relative to males with spironolactone **.
  3. In a prespecified sub-analysis of the PARAGON-HF trial, benefits of sacubitril-valsartan on the primary composite outcome (total HF hospitalizations and CV death) were sustained up to a higher LVEF in females (up to 60%) than in males (up to 45%).
  4. A meta-analysis of trials of RAAS inhibitors was consistent with this finding; the benefits of candesartan, spironolactone and sacubitril-valsartan were sustained to a higher LVEF in females than in males.
  5. There is no evidence from TOPCAT or EMPEROR-PRESERVED that there are racial differences in treatment effect of MRAs and SGLT2i’s, respectively.

Integration of Remote Monitoring and Multidisciplinary Technology

Implantable remote pulmonary artery (PA) pressure guided monitoring for patients with HFpEF, NYHA Class III symptoms and a prior hospitalization was associated with a 46% reduction in HF hospitalizations compared to routine care.

The Hemodynamic-Guided Management of HF (GUIDE-HF) trial, testing whether this benefit extended to patients with NYHA class II- IV symptoms and without a prior history hospitalization found that PA pressure guided management did not reduce the composite endpoint of all-cause mortality and total HF events.

Something interesting

  1. Patients with HFmrEF have similar pathophysiology and treatment responses as those with HFrEF.

  2. Cardiac amyloidosis, for example, is present in up to 13% of HFpEF patients on routine biopsy.

  3. A recently validated risk score can predict patients with HFpEF at risk of sudden cardiac death.

    [Adabag S, Langsetmo L. Sudden cardiac death risk prediction in heart failure with
    preserved ejection fraction. Heart Rhythm 2020;17:358–64. ]

    [doi:10.1016/j.hrthm.2019.12.009]

  4. Cardiomyocyte calcium homeostasis was markedly abnormal in diabetic and hypertensive HFpEF but not in ischemic HFpEF.

  5. Obese HFpEF patients have markedly different clinical, hemodynamic and molecular changes compared to non-obese HFpEF patients.

  6. HFpEF caused by microvascular inflammation, HFrEF caused by cardiomyocyte loss.

Conclusion

LVEF is a continuous variable, and response to HF therapies appears to be graded such that HF therapies – including beta-blockers, RAAS inhibitors, ARNI, and SGLT2i - that reduce both CV death and HF hospitalization at lower LVEFs are less effective at reducing death but continue to reduce HF hospitalization at higher LVEFs beyond 50%. This points to the noncardiac mechanisms that underpin or accompany HF as the LVEF increases, as well as differences in underlying pathophysiology.

Questions

  1. While the complex interaction of all the proposed non-cardiac mechanisms remain unclear, they appear to shift from mechanisms in HFrEF at higher ranges of LVEF, with a gradual change in phenotype and a gradual decrease in the proportion of CV causes of hospitalization and death as LVEF increases.
